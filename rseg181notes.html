Welcome to the course! Over the next ten weeks, we are going to take a detailed look at how to successfully implement agile methodologies for a software development organization. We will review the basics of Agile, with a strong focus on Scrum, but our purpose is to understand how to actually drive change and make a transition to Agile successful. My goal is to arm you with tools and knowledge that I wish I had when I took on my first Agile transformation.

I have had some unique experiences with Agile and we will share some interesting stories as we work through our course. This week we will look at the origins of Agile, why it has become so popular for software development organizations and what makes it so different from earlier methods.

I got my start in the software industry during the heyday of Waterfall. Strict Waterfall is truly cumbersome. Software moves from one phase to the next: Requirements, then Analysis, then Design, then Coding, then Testing. My first jobs were writing COBOL for large mainframe environments with strict processes. Everything had to be documented with written approvals every step of the way. After a few big corporate jobs, I ventured out on my own and ran a software consulting firm for many years. I knew that I had been frustrated with the inability to deliver software in a timely manner. I knew that I was frustrated with the bureaucracy. What I didn’t know was that my new direction would one day have a name: Agile.

As a consultant, I worked closely with my clients to design their applications. We didn’t spec everything out. Rather, I would meet with them and jot down their thoughts. We might draw some wireframes on a piece of paper. I would then code up a screen or two with fake data and zip back to the client’s office to show them what I had and get their feedback. We repeated this over and over until they had exactly what they needed.

Most of the work was done by me, sometimes with the help of a few subcontractors. It was the essence of agility, but I know now that the way I was doing it would not have been able to scale. With larger teams working on more complex systems, I would have needed more disciplined approaches and that is what the Agile we know today provides.

This week, we will explore how Agile got started. To know why agile was needed, we have to go back even further - how did Waterfall become the process of choice for software and why was Agile able to so thoroughly displace it?

In the earliest days of software development, computers were weak and had very little memory. Software had to be pretty simple. Development was done much like I did with my consulting company. Business people would sit with a programmer and together they would build out what they wanted the computer to do.

Computers grew more powerful, software needs became more complex and soon you needed a team of programmers, separate testers, technical writers to build documentation - more and more people. Before long it was taking a lot longer to build software, often what was delivered didn’t work as expected and there were tons of bugs. It became obvious that what we needed was project management. Where to turn? Who manages projects really well? Why, construction projects do. Let’s use what they use!

The only problem was that construction projects were defined projects and software is an empirical process. You will learn about these terms in your materials this week. Over time, most organizations found that they were still having the same problems. Software still took too long to build, by the time it was finished it didn’t look much like what was needed and it was full of bugs.

Development teams started looking for better ways. Kent Beck developed Extreme Programming. Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland created Scrum. These took hold and proved that there was a better way. Finally a group got together and created the Agile Manifesto. Agile continued to grow and grow until now it is the accepted best practice, although with many variations, for software development projects.
